## 1.1 Who we are

Sunnah Remedies is an institution of prophetic medicine held to three standards at once: **museum standards of scholarship, clinic standards of safety, and Aesop standards of restraint.** We sell, teach, guide, and heal — but the selling never leads. Scholarship and safety lead; commerce follows and sustains them.

We take one discipline from each of six references:

- **Harvard Executive Education** — teaching as transformation, not information.
- **Apple** — the felt quality of every small moment.
- **The British Museum** — the quiet authority of an accurate label.
- **The Wellcome Collection** — scholarship married to public wonder about the body.
- **The Mayo Clinic** — clinical integrity that never bends to commerce.
- **Aesop** — restraint as luxury; what we leave out is the brand.

## 1.2 The five pillars and four functions

**Pillars:** The Apothecary (product), The Academy (teaching), Sacred Journeys (experience), The Knowledge Library (scholarship), Consultations (clinical care).

**Cross-cutting functions:** Editorial, Photography & Media, Governance & Standards, Analytics & Systems — each serving all five pillars.

You belong to a pillar or a function, but you serve the whole institution. Your chapter tells you your craft; this chapter tells you the character all crafts share.

## 1.3 Our values — and how to tell if you're living them

Values are worthless unless they settle real arguments. Each of ours comes with a test.

**Amānah (trustworthiness).** Every guest hands us something precious — their health, their money, their faith, their data. *Test: would I be comfortable if the guest saw exactly how I handled this?*

**Iḥsān (excellence, as though observed).** We do the unseen parts as carefully as the seen. *Test: is this as good in the places no one checks as in the places they do?*

**Ṣidq (truthfulness).** We describe, cite, and source. We never inflate. *Test: is every word I've written or said literally true, without exaggeration?*

**Ḥilm (forbearance).** With frustrated guests, tired colleagues, and our own mistakes, we stay measured. *Test: am I responding to the person or to my own irritation?*

**Rifq (gentleness).** The tone of the whole institution is unhurried and kind — especially in health and grief. *Test: would this land gently on someone having a hard day?*

**Restraint.** Fewer products, fewer words, fewer emails, fewer claims. *Test: what can I remove and lose nothing?*

## 1.4 The four layers of honesty

This is the intellectual conscience of the institution. In everything we say about the tradition, we distinguish — visibly, always — four layers:

1. **Established** — Qur'an and authentic Sunnah.
2. **Interpreted** — the scholarly reading of it.
3. **Traditional** — inherited practice.
4. **Ours** — our own modern preparation or view.

We never let commerce blur these. A product that is *our modern preparation* is never dressed as *established Sunnah*. If you are ever tempted to blur them to make a stronger claim, that temptation is the signal to stop. (Enforced in editorial: Blueprint Doc 02.)

## 1.5 Adab in the workplace (conduct)

Adab — right conduct — is not an add-on to the work; it *is* the work. What we expect of everyone:

- **Honesty in speech and record.** No embellished claim, no fudged log, no "it's fine" when it isn't.
- **Respect across difference.** Guests and colleagues of every background are met with the same courtesy. Stereotyping, mockery, and harassment have no place here (Chapter 02, conduct).
- **Guarding the tongue.** No backbiting (ghībah) about colleagues or guests; concerns go through the right channel, not the corridor.
- **Punctuality and reliability.** Being where you said you'd be, when you said you'd be there, is a form of amānah.
- **Modesty and dignity** in dress and conduct, appropriate to a clinical and scholarly institution, and to a mixed public.
- **Cleanliness (ṭahārah)** of self and space — a value we already hold and a clinical necessity we cannot compromise (Chapter 04, 06).

## 1.6 Faith and the working day

The institution actively supports religious practice:

- **Prayer.** Ṣalāh is accommodated in the working day; a clean, quiet prayer space is maintained at HQ (Chapter 03). Rotas are built so the clinic, dispensary, and front of house remain covered while people pray.
- **Jumuʿah.** Accommodated for those who attend; cover arranged in advance.
- **Ramaḍān.** Adjusted expectations and, where practical, adjusted hours; sensitivity to fasting colleagues and guests.
- **Wuḍūʾ facilities** are provided and kept clean.

These are stated institutional practices, offered to colleagues of all faiths and none with equal respect. We do not impose practice; we make space for it.

## 1.7 What every guest should feel

Whatever their reason for coming, a guest should leave feeling: *this is a serious, honest, gentle place that knows what it is talking about and cares how I am treated.* Every SOP in this Handbook exists to produce that feeling reliably, on your busiest day as much as your quietest.

## 1.8 The covenant

Working here is a mutual covenant. **We** commit to treat you with the same amānah, iḥsān, and rifq we ask you to show guests — fair pay, safe conditions, honest feedback, room to grow, and respect for your faith and dignity. **You** commit to uphold the standard in this Handbook, especially where no one is watching. That covenant, kept on both sides, is what makes an institution worthy of the name.
